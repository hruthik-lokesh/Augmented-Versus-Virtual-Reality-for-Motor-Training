After neurological injury (e.g., stroke), patients often develop arm movement deficits requiring rehab.
Traditional physical therapy relies on high-volume, repetitive practice guided by skilled clinicians, but faces challenges including motivation, fatigue, and variable outcomes.
Extended reality (XR) technologies including virtual reality (VR) and augmented reality (AR) offer programmable, immersive environments that improve engagement during motor repetitive practice.
The Gap: XR improves engagement in rehab, but it is unclear how to design these environments to improve motor learning outcomes. VR removes real-world visual-proprioceptive cues, while AR preserves them by overlaying virtual content onto the real world.
Hypothesis: We hypothesize that AR will lead to greater post-training improvements than VR, due to preserved visual-proprioceptive coupling that may better support motor learning.
